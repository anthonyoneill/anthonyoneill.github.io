Spacecraft stability

A simple example of perturbation techniques is the stability of a circular orbit to small disturbances. If a satellite is in a circular orbit around the centre of the earth and is given a small radial velocity will the satellite be thrown into space, crash into the earth or is the orbit stable? General relativity is not required in order to develop a basic model of this scenario, the Newtonian model of gravity will suffice.

Newtonian model

The motion of the satellite is assumed to be planar so that the position of the satellite is given by the time varying polar coordinates \( (r(t), \theta(t)) \), with the origin of the coordinate system at the centre of the earth. In the Newtonian model the motion satisfies the nonlinear differential equations $$ \frac{d^2 r}{d t^2} - r \left( \frac{d \theta}{d t} \right)^2 = - \frac{GM}{r^2},$$ $$ \frac{1}{r} \frac{d}{dt} \left( r^2 \frac{d \theta}{d t} \right) = 0, $$ where \( G \) is the universal gravitational constant and \( M \) is the mass of the earth. These equations arise from the conservation of linear and angular momentum and are difficult to solve in general.

An obvious solution to the above set of differential equations is a circular orbit \( r = r_0 \) where \( r_0 \) is a constant. Substituting this solution into the second differential equation we find that $$ \frac{d \theta}{d t} = \frac{h}{r_0^2} = \omega, $$ where \( h \) is a constant equal to the angular momentum and \( \omega \) is the angular frequency. Then the first differential equation suggests the relation \( r_0^3 \omega^2 = GM \) which is Kepler's third law of planetary motion. This simple circular motion is all well and good but we want to know how this orbit will react when perturbed slightly as this will suggest how stable the orbit is.

Regular perturbation expansion

Which shall assume that the spacecraft is given some small radial velocity \( \epsilon v \) where \( \epsilon \ll 1 \), so the angular momentum \( h \) remains constant. The radial coordinate and the angular frequency are then given in the expanded form $$ r(t) = r_0 + \epsilon r_1(t) + \ldots, $$ $$ \frac{d \theta}{d t} = \omega + \epsilon \frac{d}{d t}\left( \theta_1(t) \right) + \ldots. $$ Substituting these expansions into our differential equations gives $$ \epsilon \frac{d^2 r_1}{d t^2} + \ldots - \left( r_0 + \epsilon r_1 + \ldots \right) \left( \omega + \epsilon \frac{d \theta_1}{d t} + \ldots \right)^2 = -\frac{GM}{\left( r_0 + \epsilon r_1 + \ldots \right)^2}, $$ $$ \left( r_0 + \epsilon r_1 + \ldots \right)^2 \left( \omega + \epsilon \frac{d \theta_1}{d t} + \ldots \right) = h = r_0^2 \omega. $$

Using Newton's binomial theorem the right-hand side term in the first equation may be written as $$ \begin{align} -\frac{GM}{\left( r_0 + \epsilon r_1 + \ldots \right)^2} =& -\frac{GM}{r_0^2 \left( 1 + \frac{\epsilon r_1}{r_0} + \ldots \right)^2} \\ =& - \frac{GM}{r_0^2} \left( 1 - \frac{2 \epsilon r_1}{r_0} + O(\epsilon^2) \right) \\ =& - r_0 \omega^2 + 2 \epsilon \omega^2 r_1 + O(\epsilon^2), \end{align} $$ since \( r_0^3 \omega^2 = GM \). So expanding the left-hand side of the equation too we find that $$ \epsilon \frac{d^2 r_1}{d t^2} - r_0 \omega^2 - \epsilon \omega^2 r_1 - 2 \epsilon r_0 \omega \frac{d \theta_1}{d t} = - r_0 \omega^2 + 2 \epsilon \omega^2 r_1 + O(\epsilon^2). $$ Similarly expanding the second equation we have $$ r_0^2 \omega + 2 \epsilon r_0 r_1 \omega + \epsilon r_0^2 \frac{d \theta_1}{d t} = r_0^2 \omega + O(\epsilon^2). $$ Cleary these equations are satisfied at leading order then \( O(\epsilon) \) we have two differential equations, governing the first-order perturbation, given by $$ \frac{d^2 r_1}{d t^2} - 3 \omega^2 r_1 - 2 r_0 \omega \frac{d \theta_1}{d t} = 0, $$ $$ 2 r_0 r_1 \omega + r_0^2 \frac{d \theta_1}{d t} = 0 \Rightarrow \frac{d \theta_1}{d t} = -\frac{2 r_1 \omega}{r_0}. $$ Substituting the second equation into the first we find that $$ \frac{d^2 r_1}{d t^2} + \omega^2 r_1 = 0. $$ This is the equation for simple harmonic motion and has a solution of the form $$ r_1(t) = c_1 \cos(\omega t) + c_2 \sin(\omega t), $$ where \( c_1 \) and \( c_2 \) are constants. This solution for \( r_1 \) implies that $$ \begin{align} \theta_1 =& - \frac{2 \omega}{r_0} \int r_1(t) dt \\ =& \frac{2}{r_0} \left[ c_2 \cos(\omega t) - c_1 \sin(\omega t) \right] + \text{constant}. \end{align} $$

Conclusions

Since \( r_1(t) \) oscillates without growing or decaying the orbit is stable to small velocity perturbations. The frequency of the oscillation is equal to the original frequency of the circular orbit, this means that the perturbed orbit is slightly elliptical with the centre of the earth at one focus. So we don't need to worry about our satellite being thrown out into space or crashing into the earth due to small changes in their velocity.

Asymptotic techniques offer useful insights into many complex problems by simplifying the governing equations. This simple example is a regular expansion which is uniformly valid, this is not always the case as in some situations the asymptotic scaling may break down in certain regions of the domain. By rescaling the equations to find solutions in different regions and matching across the domain this difficulty can be overcome. Many fluid mechanics problems can be tackled using asymptotic techniques along with problems involving coexisting slow and fast timescales such as biological processes, chemical reactions and mechanical systems.
